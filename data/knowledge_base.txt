## Section 1: Large Language Models (LLMs)

Large Language Models (LLMs) are a type of artificial intelligence model designed to understand and generate human-like text. They are built using deep learning techniques, particularly the transformer architecture introduced in the paper "Attention is All You Need" by Vaswani et al. in 2017.

LLMs are trained on massive datasets containing text from books, websites, and other sources. During training, the model learns to predict the next word in a sequence, which allows it to generate coherent and contextually appropriate text.

Popular LLMs include GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) by OpenAI, BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) by Google, and LLaMA by Meta. These models have billions of parameters that store learned patterns from the training data.

The transformer architecture uses self-attention mechanisms to weigh the importance of different words in a sentence relative to each other. This allows the model to capture long-range dependencies and understand context effectively.

LLMs can perform various natural language processing tasks including text generation, translation, summarization, question answering, and sentiment analysis. However, they have limitations including the tendency to generate plausible-sounding but incorrect information, known as hallucination.

## Section 2: Hallucination in AI

Hallucination in the context of Large Language Models refers to the phenomenon where the model generates information that appears plausible and confident but is factually incorrect, nonsensical, or not grounded in its training data or any reliable source.

There are several types of hallucinations in LLMs:
1. Factual Hallucination: The model states incorrect facts, such as wrong dates, names, or statistics.
2. Fabrication: The model invents entities, events, or information that do not exist.
3. Inconsistency: The model contradicts itself within the same response or across responses.
4. Reasoning Errors: The model makes logical errors in its reasoning process.

Hallucinations occur because LLMs are trained to generate statistically likely text, not necessarily true text. The model learns patterns from its training data but does not have a true understanding of facts or the ability to verify information.

Factors that increase hallucination risk include:
- Queries about topics with limited training data
- Questions requiring precise numerical data
- Requests for information about recent events
- Complex multi-step reasoning tasks
- Prompts that encourage creative or speculative responses

## Section 3: Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)

Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique that enhances the capabilities of Large Language Models by combining them with external knowledge retrieval systems. RAG was introduced by Facebook AI Research (now Meta AI) in 2020.

In a RAG system, when a query is received, the system first retrieves relevant documents or passages from an external knowledge base. These retrieved documents are then provided as context to the LLM, which generates a response based on both the query and the retrieved information.

The RAG architecture consists of two main components:
1. Retriever: Searches an external knowledge base to find relevant documents. This typically uses semantic search with vector embeddings.
2. Generator: An LLM that generates responses using the query and retrieved context.

RAG helps reduce hallucinations by grounding the model's responses in retrieved factual information. Instead of relying solely on learned patterns, the model can reference actual documents to generate its response.

Vector databases are commonly used in RAG systems to store document embeddings. Popular vector databases include ChromaDB, Pinecone, Weaviate, and Milvus. These databases enable efficient similarity search to find the most relevant documents for a given query.

The embedding model converts text into numerical vectors that capture semantic meaning. Similar texts have vectors that are close together in the embedding space. Common embedding models include sentence-transformers, OpenAI embeddings, and BERT-based models.

## Section 4: Hallucination Detection Methods

Hallucination detection involves identifying when an LLM generates information that is not supported by reliable sources or contradicts known facts. Several approaches exist for detecting hallucinations:

1. Claim Extraction and Verification: This method extracts individual factual claims from the LLM's response and verifies each claim against a knowledge base or external sources. Claims that cannot be verified are flagged as potential hallucinations.

2. Natural Language Inference (NLI): NLI models can determine the relationship between two texts - entailment, contradiction, or neutral. By comparing the LLM's output with retrieved evidence, NLI can detect contradictions that indicate hallucination.

3. Self-Consistency Checking: By generating multiple responses to the same query and comparing them, inconsistencies can be detected. High variance across responses may indicate hallucination.

4. Knowledge Graph Verification: Factual claims can be checked against structured knowledge graphs like Wikidata or DBpedia to verify their accuracy.

5. Source Attribution: Requiring the LLM to cite sources and verifying those citations can help identify unsupported claims.

The hallucination score typically ranges from 0 to 1, where:
- 0.0-0.3: Low hallucination risk, response is well-grounded in evidence
- 0.3-0.7: Medium risk, some claims may lack support
- 0.7-1.0: High hallucination risk, response contains unsupported or contradictory information

## Section 6: Machine Learning Fundamentals

Machine learning is a subset of artificial intelligence that enables computers to learn from data without being explicitly programmed. There are three main types of machine learning:

1. Supervised Learning: The model learns from labeled data, where each example has an input and a known output. Examples include classification and regression tasks.

2. Unsupervised Learning: The model finds patterns in unlabeled data. Examples include clustering and dimensionality reduction.

3. Reinforcement Learning: The model learns through trial and error, receiving rewards or penalties for its actions.

Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that uses neural networks with many layers (hence "deep"). Transformers, the architecture behind LLMs, are a type of deep learning model.

Word embeddings are dense vector representations of words that capture semantic meaning. Word2Vec and GloVe were early embedding techniques. Modern approaches use contextual embeddings from models like BERT, which create different representations for the same word depending on its context.

## Section 7: Natural Language Processing (NLP)

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a field of AI focused on the interaction between computers and human language. Key NLP tasks include:

1. Tokenization: Breaking text into words, subwords, or characters
2. Named Entity Recognition (NER): Identifying entities like names, dates, and locations
3. Part-of-Speech Tagging: Labeling words with their grammatical categories
4. Sentiment Analysis: Determining the emotional tone of text
5. Text Classification: Categorizing text into predefined categories
6. Question Answering: Generating answers to questions based on context
7. Text Summarization: Creating concise summaries of longer texts
8. Machine Translation: Translating text between languages

Modern NLP is dominated by transformer-based models that have achieved state-of-the-art results across these tasks. Pre-trained models like BERT, GPT, and T5 can be fine-tuned for specific applications.

## Section 8: Vector Databases and Semantic Search

Vector databases are specialized databases designed to store, index, and query high-dimensional vector embeddings. They are essential for building RAG systems and other semantic search applications.

Key concepts in vector databases:
1. Embeddings: Numerical vector representations of data (text, images, etc.)
2. Similarity Search: Finding vectors closest to a query vector
3. Distance Metrics: Methods to measure vector similarity (cosine similarity, Euclidean distance)
4. Indexing: Data structures that enable efficient similarity search (HNSW, IVF)

ChromaDB is an open-source embedding database that is easy to use and suitable for local development. It stores embeddings with associated metadata and documents, enabling efficient similarity search.

The process of building a vector search system involves:
1. Loading documents from various sources
2. Splitting documents into smaller chunks
3. Generating embeddings for each chunk
4. Storing embeddings in a vector database
5. Querying with a new input to find similar documents

## Section 9: Prompt Engineering

Prompt engineering is the practice of designing effective prompts to get desired outputs from LLMs. Good prompts are clear, specific, and provide appropriate context.

Key prompt engineering techniques:
1. Zero-shot prompting: Asking the model to perform a task without examples
2. Few-shot prompting: Providing examples to guide the model's behavior
3. Chain-of-thought prompting: Encouraging step-by-step reasoning
4. System prompts: Setting the model's role and behavior constraints

For RAG systems, effective prompts include:
- Clear instructions to use only the provided context
- Explicit instruction to admit when information is not available
- Request for source attribution when possible

## Section 10: Evaluation Metrics

Evaluating hallucination detection systems requires appropriate metrics:

1. Precision: The proportion of detected hallucinations that are actual hallucinations
2. Recall: The proportion of actual hallucinations that are detected
3. F1 Score: The harmonic mean of precision and recall
4. Accuracy: The proportion of correctly classified instances

For RAG systems, additional metrics include:
- Retrieval accuracy: How often relevant documents are retrieved
- Answer relevance: How well the generated answer addresses the query
- Faithfulness: How well the answer is grounded in the retrieved context

Human evaluation remains important for assessing the quality of hallucination detection, as automated metrics may not capture all nuances of factual accuracy.

1. **Size**: They can grow up to 100 feet (30 meters) long and weigh as much as 200 tons (approximately 180 metric tonnes).
2. **Diet**: They are filter feeders, primarily consuming tiny shrimp-like creatures called krill, eating up to 4 tons per day during feeding season.

5. **Conservation**: Once heavily hunted, they are now protected but remain endangered, with an estimated population of 10,000–25,000.
